# Lead players from one simple causal feedbackloop to a systems understanding

## The system

It is very difficult to provide templates for the system debrief, because whatever comments, insights and learnings occur should be tied to the results of the play. Since these results happen on the spot, we cannot pre-analyze them. You have to do that *on the fly*.

However, one result is often unspoken *in the room*, namely the **expected** result. Players took decisions with the expectation that something would result, and were then met with the actual results, which often differ from their expectations.

It may make sense to have participants draw their expectations on top of the actual results. You can start this process by asking: Are the results what you expected? If not, can you draw your expectations on top of the results? 

If you do this, you should either project the result on a whiteboard where participants can draw in their expected curves, or by printing copies of the game results on paper as handouts. Printing is more work for you, but may be preferrable since, first, participants do not have to draw their expectations *in public* in front of all the others and, second, they can take the piece of paper they drew on with them home and can thus re-visit the discrepancy repeatedly and privately. And talk about with friends and family - fat chance :) This type of analysis often leads to causal questions, and, at best, causal explanations.

## Changing habits exercise

We are not only social beings, but we are also habitual ones. We do things not because we thought about them and found them right, useful, sensible, we often do things just out of habit. For example, our social systems of inequality exists not just because some benefit immensely and, therefore, do everything to keep it that way, it also exists because we all play along, out of habit. Same with our energy system, same with our food, poverty and (lack of) empowerment.
To drive the power of habits home, you may want to play this little exercise, which [Dennis Meadows](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jm9vxWX-9qA) often plays and, as far as I can tell, invented it:

1. Ask people to cross their arms – and keep them crossed. Do that yourself, visible to all.
1. Ask people to make a mental note of which arm was on top. Help them by saying: *For me, my left arm is on top.* (or right arm, whichever was the case) 
1. Ask them to uncross their arms. Do that yourself for all to see.
1. Ask them to cross their arms again, but this time putting the other arm on top. Do it yourself. It does not come natural. There will be some commotion in the room, people will think, but in the end succeed. Give them the time they need.
1. *Debrief:* Changing habits is not easy. However, 1) you can do it, 2) you have to think about it, 3) it takes practice.
