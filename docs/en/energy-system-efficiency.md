# Energy system efficiency

We are getting ever more efficient in using energy. This means, that for the same amount of output, we need to put less and less energy in. ([The big exception is agriculture](the-energy-cost-of-feeding-the-world.md)). 

The simplest example is the milage we get while driving a car. While the [most fuel efficient car in the US](https://www.visualcapitalist.com/the-most-fuel-efficient-cars-from-1975-to-today/) in 1975 got 28.5 miles to the gallon (mpg), in 2023 it got 140 mpg. If you consider the entire car fleet, the improvement is [much less impressive](https://news.umich.edu/fuel-efficiency-of-vehicles-on-the-road-little-progress-since-the-1920s/) (Extra points: Any ides why?). 

What is true for cars is true for all our use of energy. Over the years, we've gotten [more and more efficient](https://ourworldindata.org/energy). With this policy you decide how much more we'll improve our efficiency in the **future:** By 1, or 2 or 2.5% per year? (For comparison: 1% per yr is the historical value over the last 40 years.) Beware of [the power of compound interest](https://www.rathbones.com/en-gb/wealth-management/knowledge-and-insight/the-magic-of-compound-interest!).
