# Emissions and temperature

One discussion takes place quite often: Why does the temperature stay so high even when emissions fall so low?
The question and surprise stems from an incomplete mental model. Most of us have learned that it is the GHG (green-house gas) emissions that cause higher temperatures. This mental model of the cause and effect is so deeply embedded by now that we fight tooth and nail about reaching **net zero**.
![escimo1](../assets/images/escimo1.png)  

The physical reality is, however, a bit more involved. First, the GHGs cause higher temperatures by absorbing heat that otherwise would have escaped to deep space, i.e., they block some heat (technically parts of the long-wave (LW) radiation) from escaping. This distinction becomes crucial later on.
![escimo2](../assets/images/escimo2.png)  

So what, you may say. OK, the causality goes through an intermediary variable, but still, less GHG emissions, less blocking, less temperature – all arrows are blue.

## The albedo loop
True, but temperature is embedded in a larger physical system. First, a higher temperature darkens the planet, mostly through the melting of ice, on oceans and land. A darker planet absorbs more short-wave radiation from the sun, thus raising the temperature. This is called [the **albedo** feedback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice%E2%80%93albedo_feedback). A first self-reinforcing feedback loop (all arrows are blue) acting independently of any GHG emissions!
![escimo3](../assets/images/escimo3.png) 

## The moisture loop
Second, higher temperatures lead to the atmosphere being able to hold more moisture. Moisture is H2O, a very potent gas that blocks more long-wave radiation escaping to space, raising the temperature even more. A second self-reinforcing feedback loop (all arrows are blue) acting independently of any GHG emissions! For more, look here: [The water vapor feedback](https://science.nasa.gov/earth/climate-change/steamy-relationships-how-atmospheric-water-vapor-amplifies-earths-greenhouse-effect/)
![escimo4](../assets/images/escimo4.png)  

## The permafrost melting loop
Third, higher temperatures lead to more permafrost melting. Since permafrost is a large (~ 1500 Giga tonnes) store of CH4 and CO2, this will get released into the atmosphere over time, blocking more long-wave radiation escaping to space, raising the temperature even more. A third self-reinforcing feedback loop (all arrows are blue) acting independently of any GHG emissions! Check out the [permafrost feedback](https://www.esa.int/Applications/Observing_the_Earth/FutureEO/Permafrost_thaw_it_s_complicated).
![escimo5](../assets/images/escimo5.png) 

The three loops sketched here are in reality more complicated, especially the moisture loop since it involves clouds of which we know very little so far, but the causality shown above holds true. Thus, even if we reach net zero faster than we think, there are powerful feedbacks that keep our planet warmer than it has been for a long, long, long time. 

## We are in a different world, baby!
Why? The climate system now is not what it was 200 years ago before the industrial revolution. Relentlessly emitting GHGs for 200 years has created a different system where different feedback causalities dominate the behavior of the system. An analogy might be that of an alcoholic. After drinking excessively for a lifetime, her of his body is no longer physiologically what it was before he or she took the first drink. Financial analysts call this a regime shift. Alcoholics call this addiction.

So, is it too late? Since all loops are self-reinforcing? Strengthening the vicious cycles with every turn they take?

## The black body radiation loop
Not really, because there is one powerful **balancing** loop at work – again completely independent of what we humans may or may not do – pure physics: [The Stefan Boltzmann black body radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stefan%E2%80%93Boltzmann_law). Planet earth is, in radiative energy terms, a black body. And black bodies radiate heat outward (in the case of our planet, to deep space) not just in proportion to their temperature, but in proportion to the **fourth power**  of their temperature. Hence, anything we humans can do to help this feedback is well worth doing.
