# The Energy Cost of Feeding the World

How much energy does it take to produce the food we eat? The answer has changed dramatically over the last 200 years — and not in a good direction.

#### Traditional Farming

For most of human history, farming was powered by the sun. Seeds, rain, human muscle and animal power were the inputs. The energy return was positive: for every calorie of effort put in, farmers harvested roughly 5 to 10 calories of food. This ratio is called the Energy Return on Investment (**EROI**).

#### The Industrial Turn

From around 1900 onwards, and accelerating after World War II, farming became industrialised. Tractors replaced horses, synthetic fertilisers replaced manure, pesticides replaced manual weeding, and irrigation pumps replaced rain. All of these inputs run on fossil fuels — oil, gas and coal.

The result: the EROI of agriculture flipped. Today, modern industrial farming typically requires 5 to 15 calories of fossil fuel energy to produce a single calorie of food on your plate — when you count the full chain from field to fork: fuel for machinery, energy to produce fertiliser, pesticides, irrigation, transport, processing and refrigeration.

#### Why Fertiliser Matters So Much

The single biggest energy cost is nitrogen fertiliser. Producing it requires the Haber-Bosch process, which uses natural gas as both a raw material and an energy source. Roughly 1–2 litres of diesel equivalent are needed to produce just 1 kg of nitrogen fertiliser. Without synthetic nitrogen, the world could not feed its current population — but the energy price is enormous.

#### The general shape of the development over time:

***Traditional/subsistence farming:***
EROI is positive — you get more out than you put in
Roughly 5–10 calories out per calorie in (human/animal labor, simple tools)

***Modern industrial farming:***
EROI is negative — you put in more than you get out
Roughly 10 calories in per 1 calorie of food out when you count everything: diesel for machinery, natural gas for fertilizer production ([Haber-Bosch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haber_process) is hugely energy intensive), pesticides, irrigation, transport, processing, refrigeration

- Pre-1900: EROI ~ 5–10:1 (positive)
- 1950s: roughly breaking even
- 1970s onwards: clearly negative, ~ 3–10:1 input/output depending on crop and region
- Today: up to 10–15:1 for heavily processed food

#### What This Means for the Game

In SimFuture, the Minister for Food and Agriculture faces exactly this dilemma. Investments in food security are not free — they carry an energy cost, and that energy cost has consequences for climate and inequality. A region that can shift towards less energy-intensive farming — through soil health, reduced meat consumption, or precision agriculture — can reduce both its emissions and its vulnerability to fossil fuel price shocks.
