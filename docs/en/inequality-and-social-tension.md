# Inequality and social tension

Rising inequality leads to social tension. How much and how fast, differs across time and cultures. Hence the variable *tolerated inequality*. But in the end, there is no denying that [inequality leads to social tension](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2020/jan/24/most-political-unrest-has-one-big-root-cause-soaring-inequality). 

In such situations, a perfectly normal reaction for any individual or any group in society is to look out for themselves. Finding answers to the question **what’s in it for me?** leads to more inequality, fueling even more social tension, and on and on. There are many colloquial ways of describing such a downward spiral. From the benign *when it rains it pours* all the way to [this is no technological breakdown, this is the road to hell](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OcW-BSEB3ng). 

Vicious cycles by themselves do not have the resources to break out of their cycle. That energy has to come from outside: From something or someone working against the inequality. From someone or something providing a goal or vision larger than that of any special interest. There are situations where none of this happens. This leads to collapse and, at best, a [rebuilding from sheer exhaustion](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/6034091).

If you find your players are talking about, or trying to understand this feedback process, ask them for personal examples of having been in, or witnessed others in such a loop. Ask for examples from novels or movies. Ask them also for examples of outside interventions to break the downward causality of this loop; what happened? Ask them if they can think about a social situation with *too little inequality*, i.e., **too much equality**.
